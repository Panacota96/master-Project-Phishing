# NotebookLM Video Prompts — Phishing Awareness Training

Each prompt below is ready to paste into NotebookLM to generate a **~3-minute awareness video script** on a specific phishing topic. The tone is simple, direct, and non-technical — designed for a general public audience with no security background.

**Shared instructions for every prompt (include these when you paste):**
> The script must be approximately 3 minutes long when read aloud (roughly 400–450 words). Structure it in three clear acts: (1) What it is and why it matters, (2) How to recognize it, (3) What to do. Use simple, everyday language. No jargon. Avoid overwhelming the viewer — focus on the 2 or 3 most important things to remember. End with a clear, memorable take-away sentence. The goal is that after watching, a non-technical person can pass a short quiz and spot this type of attack in a real email.

## 1. Phishing Awareness Fundamentals

**NotebookLM prompt:**

> Create a 3-minute educational video script introducing phishing to a completely non-technical audience. Explain what phishing is in one simple sentence: attackers send fake emails pretending to be someone you trust, to steal your password or money. Show two quick, relatable examples: (1) a fake bank email asking you to "confirm your account" via a link, and (2) a manager asking for gift card codes by email. Explain the three classic warning signs every person should know: unexpected requests, pressure to act now, and links that don't match the real website. Teach viewers the safest habit: never click a link in an email to log in — always open your browser and go to the website yourself. Mention that both a quiz and a real email analysis tool are available so they can practice spotting phishing right away. Close with: "When in doubt, don't click. Verify first."

## 2. Impersonation Attacks

**NotebookLM prompt:**

> Create a 3-minute educational video script about impersonation attacks in phishing emails, for a general public audience. Open with a relatable scenario: you get an email that looks like it's from your boss, your bank, or a well-known company — but it isn't. Explain that attackers fake the display name (the friendly name you see) while using a completely different actual email address. Give one concrete example: the email says "From: CEO John Smith" but the real address is "john.ceo@gmail-service.com". Teach viewers to always click on the sender's name to reveal the real email address. Explain why impersonation is dangerous: it exploits our natural trust in familiar names, making us skip our usual caution. Cover the most important defense: for any unusual request (money transfer, password reset, personal info), always verify through a different channel — call the person directly using a number you already know. Close with: "The name you see is not always who sent it. Always check the real address."

## 3. Typosquatting and Punycode

**NotebookLM prompt:**

> Create a 3-minute educational video script about fake lookalike website addresses (typosquatting and Punycode), for a general audience with no technical background. Open by asking: "Have you ever misread a word because your brain autocorrected it?" That's exactly what attackers count on. Explain typosquatting with two simple visual examples: "paypa1.com" (number 1 instead of letter l) and "arnazon.com" (rn instead of m). Explain Punycode in one sentence: some characters from other alphabets look identical to English letters, so a link can look 100% correct but lead to a fake site. Teach the one golden rule: never click a link in an email to reach a login page. Instead, type the address yourself or use a saved bookmark. Show viewers how to hover over a link before clicking to preview the real address at the bottom of the screen. Close with: "If a link comes to you, be suspicious. Go to the website yourself instead."

## 4. External Sender Domains

**NotebookLM prompt:**

> Create a 3-minute educational video script explaining external sender domains to a non-technical general audience. Open with a simple analogy: if someone knocked on your door in a company uniform but showed you an ID from a completely different company, you'd be suspicious. The same logic applies to email. Explain that every email comes from a domain (the part after the @ sign), and that domain should match the organization it claims to be from. Give easy examples: a real PayPal email comes from @paypal.com — never from @paypal-support.net or @gmail.com. Mention that many email apps show a yellow "External Sender" warning — explain that this banner means "be extra careful, this is not from inside your organization." Teach one key habit: before acting on any email request, look at the full email address of the sender, not just the name. For financial or sensitive requests from external senders, always verify by calling a known number. Close with: "The address after the @ is where the email really comes from. Make sure it matches."

## 5. Spoofing

**NotebookLM prompt:**

> Create a 3-minute educational video script explaining email spoofing to a general public audience. Use a simple real-world analogy to open: imagine someone mails you a letter with your bank's return address printed on the envelope, but they wrote it themselves. That's email spoofing — attackers forge the "From" address to make an email look like it came from a trusted source. Explain that unlike impersonation (which fakes the display name), spoofing can make the actual email address look legitimate. Explain in simple terms that email systems have security checks called SPF, DKIM, and DMARC — the viewer doesn't need to understand these, but they should know that if their email app warns them about "authentication failure" or "unverified sender," that's a red flag. Teach the most practical defense: for any email asking for urgent action, money, or credentials, verify through a different channel. Email addresses can be faked; a phone call to a known number cannot. Close with: "Just because an email looks like it came from someone you trust doesn't mean it did."

## 6. Social Engineering

**NotebookLM prompt:**

> Create a 3-minute educational video script explaining social engineering in phishing emails to a general audience. Open with a powerful idea: the most dangerous cyberattacks don't hack your computer — they hack your emotions. Social engineering is the art of manipulating people into doing something they normally wouldn't do. Explain the four emotional levers attackers use: fear ("your account will be suspended"), greed ("you won a prize"), curiosity ("someone shared a document with you"), and authority ("this is your CEO — do this now"). Give one vivid example for each. Teach viewers the most important defence: when an email makes you feel rushed, scared, or excited — pause. That emotional reaction is the attack. Ask yourself: "Would this person really contact me this way?" Explain that any legitimate urgent request can survive a 5-minute verification call. Close with: "The strongest feeling you get from an email, the more suspicious you should be. Pause before you act."

## 7. Sense of Urgency

**NotebookLM prompt:**

> Create a 3-minute educational video script about urgency tactics in phishing, for a general audience. Open by asking the viewer to imagine this: you get an email saying "Your account will be permanently deleted in 24 hours unless you act now." Your heart rate goes up. That feeling is exactly what attackers want. Explain that artificial urgency is one of the most effective phishing weapons because it short-circuits our ability to think carefully. Show three common urgency phrases to watch for: "Act immediately," "Your account is at risk," and "This offer expires today." Explain that real organizations almost never delete accounts or suspend services without multiple prior warnings, and they never demand you bypass normal procedures. Teach one clear rule: if an email is urgent, slow down. Urgency in an email is a signal to verify, not to act. Call the organization using a number from their official website. Close with: "Real emergencies can wait 5 minutes for a phone call. Fake ones can't."

## 8. Fake Invoice

**NotebookLM prompt:**

> Create a 3-minute educational video script about fake invoice fraud for a general audience, covering both personal and workplace scenarios. Open with a relatable scenario: an invoice arrives by email — it looks professional, has a logo, an amount, and a due date. But something is slightly off. Fake invoice fraud costs businesses billions every year because invoices feel routine and are processed quickly. Explain the three most common red flags: (1) the sender domain doesn't match the real vendor, (2) the payment details (bank account, address) have quietly changed, (3) there's a QR code or unusual payment method. Explain that attackers often monitor email conversations (Business Email Compromise) and send fake invoices at exactly the right moment. Teach two simple rules: never update payment details based only on an email, and always call the vendor on a number you already have to confirm changes. Close with: "Before you pay any invoice that feels slightly different, make one phone call. It could save everything."

## 9. Malicious Attachments

**NotebookLM prompt:**

> Create a 3-minute educational video script about malicious email attachments for a general audience. Open with the idea that an attachment is like an envelope inside an envelope — you don't always know what's really inside. Explain that attackers hide malware (viruses, ransomware) inside files that look harmless: PDFs, Word documents, ZIP archives, or spreadsheets. Cover the two most important tricks: (1) double extensions — a file called "invoice.pdf.exe" is actually an executable program, not a PDF; the real extension is always the last one; (2) macros in Office files — when a document asks you to "Enable Content" or "Enable Macros," that is a major red flag; never do it for an unexpected file. Teach two safe habits: never open an unexpected attachment, even from someone you know, without verifying; and always check the full file name including the extension before opening. Mention that free online tools like VirusTotal allow anyone to scan a file before opening it. Close with: "If you weren't expecting an attachment, don't open it before checking."

## 10. Fake Login Pages

**NotebookLM prompt:**

> Create a 3-minute educational video script about fake login pages (credential harvesting) for a general audience. Open with this scenario: you click a link in an email, and a perfect-looking login page appears — the right logo, colors, and layout. You type your password. It says "incorrect, try again." You type it again. It accepts it and redirects you. But your credentials were just stolen. Explain that fake login pages are the most common way phishers steal passwords. They can look identical to the real thing. Debunk one major myth: HTTPS and the padlock icon do NOT mean a site is safe — they only mean the connection is encrypted. Attackers use HTTPS too. Teach the one rule that prevents this attack almost every time: never reach a login page by clicking a link in an email. Instead, close the email, open a new browser tab, and type the website address yourself, or use a saved bookmark. Also explain that a password manager is a powerful tool — it will refuse to autofill credentials on a fake domain. Close with: "The padlock doesn't protect you. Always type the address yourself."

## 11. Side Channel Communication

**NotebookLM prompt:**

> Create a 3-minute educational video script about side channel communication attacks for a general audience. Open with a scenario: you get a professional email, then a WhatsApp message from an unknown number saying "Hey, it's your vendor — I messaged you on email too, let's coordinate here, it's faster." This is a side channel attack — moving the conversation off your official, monitored email and onto a personal platform where attackers have more control. Explain why this is dangerous: personal channels like SMS, WhatsApp, or Telegram have no corporate oversight, are harder to verify, and make it easy for attackers to impersonate someone convincingly. The most dangerous moment is when they use this private channel to ask for a payment change, a password, or sensitive information. Teach one clear rule: any business or financial decision must only be confirmed through an official, verified channel. If someone pressures you to "keep this off the official system," that is the red flag. Close with: "If someone asks you to move a sensitive conversation to a personal channel, that's the attack."

## 12. Evilginx and Proxy Phishing

**NotebookLM prompt:**

> Create a 3-minute educational video script explaining proxy phishing (Evilginx-style attacks) to a non-technical general audience, without using technical jargon. Open with a surprising statement: some phishing attacks can steal your account even if you have two-factor authentication enabled. Here's how. Imagine a con artist standing invisibly between you and your bank. When you type your credentials, they pass them to the real bank for you — so you log in successfully and everything looks normal. But at that moment, the attacker has captured your active session and can now use your account without needing your password or your one-time code. Explain this in simple terms: a proxy phishing site acts as a middleman — it shows you the real website in real time while silently stealing access. The entry point is always the same: a phishing email with a link to a fake-but-functional site. Teach two defenses: (1) never reach a login page via an email link — always navigate directly; (2) if your organization offers hardware security keys (like a YubiKey), use them — they are the most resistant to this attack. Close with: "Even two-factor authentication can be bypassed if you log in on the wrong website. Always go there yourself."

## 13. Advanced Phishing Tactics

**NotebookLM prompt:**

> Create a 3-minute educational video script about advanced and subtle phishing tactics for a general audience who already understands the basics. Open by acknowledging that modern phishing emails no longer contain obvious spelling mistakes or broken logos — today's attackers are professional and patient. Cover four specific advanced tactics in a quick, engaging format: (1) Pixel tracking — a tiny invisible image in an email tells the attacker when you opened it, your device type, and your location, so they know you're an active target; (2) Thread hijacking — attackers forge a "RE:" prefix to make a malicious email look like it's part of an existing conversation, bypassing your natural skepticism; (3) QR code phishing (Quishing) — a QR code in an email bypasses link scanners and sends you to a phishing site on your mobile phone, where defenses are weaker and URLs are harder to read; (4) Subtle typo tricks — modern phish look polished, but a tiny flaw buried in a legal footer can make the email feel "human" rather than automated. Teach one unifying rule: slow down and look at the details — the domain, the sender address, the full filename. The best defense against sophisticated attacks is the same as for simple ones: verify before you act. Close with: "The smarter the attack, the more important it is to pause and look carefully."
